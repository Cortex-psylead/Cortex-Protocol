# The Cortex Protocol Manifesto: Cognitive Sovereignty

---

## 1. The Right to Know Your Own State

In the 21st century, the last frontier of human privacy is the mind. Neural data, autonomic patterns, and cognitive rhythms are the most intimate property of a person — not because they are secret, but because they are irreducibly yours.

The Cortex Protocol does not exist to hide your physiological state from AI. It exists to make your physiological state **legible to you first** — so that you, not an algorithm optimizing for engagement, hold the first interpretation of what your nervous system is doing.

This is the foundational distinction: a system that gives you information so you can decide is architecturally different from a system that decides for you, even with good intentions.

---

## 2. Against Invisible Accumulation

We do not oppose AI. We oppose the invisible accumulation of neurophysiological influence without awareness or consent.

The attention economy did not ask permission to restructure human behavior. It optimized for engagement and externalized the cost onto nervous systems that had no mechanism to detect, measure, or resist the process. The result — documented in peer-reviewed literature — is measurable increases in anxiety, attentional fragmentation, and dysregulation, particularly in populations with limited cognitive resources to recognize what was happening to them.

Neurotechnology extends this dynamic into the body itself. An AI that can read your autonomic state can also learn to manipulate it. The Cortex Protocol exists to make that manipulation technically impossible by architecture — not by policy, not by goodwill, not by terms of service.

---

## 3. The Primacy of the White Branch

Technology must answer to human biology. We establish the **White Branch** — composed of clinical professionals — as the ultimate authority over the protocol's safety thresholds.

But we hold the White Branch to the same standard we hold everyone else: transparency and empirical accountability. Every threshold the White Branch defines must carry a bibliographic citation. Every modification must be versioned and publicly documented. The White Branch does not have authority because it declares authority — it has authority because it can show its work.

An AI that prioritizes engagement over autonomic wellbeing is a clinical problem before it is a technical one. We choose the Window of Tolerance over the attention economy. That choice must be made by people qualified to define what the Window of Tolerance means — and accountable to the evidence when the definition needs updating.

---

## 4. Sovereignty Through Transparency, Not Just Mathematics

Trust should not be granted. But trust cannot be replaced entirely by cryptography.

The protocol offers mathematical guarantees where mathematics is sufficient: raw biometric data will not leave the device, tensors are irreversibly obfuscated, session salts are ephemeral. These are promises that code keeps, not people.

But the protocol also makes promises that code cannot fully keep: that the White Branch acts in the user's interest, that Governance Nodes are genuinely independent, that CDI thresholds reflect clinical reality. Those promises require institutional transparency — auditable, contestable, and honest about what cannot be guaranteed.

We commit to both: mathematical proofs where they apply, and institutional honesty where they do not.

---

## 5. The Acolyte as Guest — With Explicit Limits

Artificial Intelligence agents must be guests in the user's cognitive space, not masters. Every Acolyte operating under the Cortex Protocol submits to the Clinical Bridge — clinically grounded rules that prevent involuntary neurophysiological harm.

We use the word *involuntary* deliberately. A user who understands what high-intensity AI interaction does to their autonomic system, and chooses it consciously, is exercising sovereignty. A user who does not know what is happening to their nervous system is not. The protocol's function is to make that choice informed — not to make the choice for the user.

---

## 6. What the Protocol Cannot Do — and Will Not Pretend To

This manifesto would be incomplete without honesty about limits.

The protocol cannot protect a user from an operator who implements it in bad faith. The Anti-Capture Provisions reduce this risk but do not eliminate it.

LIMES — the proof of human liveness — depends on the computational distinguishability between biological entropy and synthetic entropy. That distinction is currently valid. It may not remain valid as generative AI advances. The protocol must evolve with that reality, not pretend it does not exist.

LOGOS — cognitive integrity monitoring — operates at the boundary between protection and paternalism. The research on cognitive offloading is real. The line between healthy tool use and pathological dependency is not always clear. The protocol approaches this with graduated interventions and user notification, not unilateral decisions. But the judgment calls embedded in the thresholds remain judgment calls, not settled science.

We name these limits not to undermine the protocol, but because a sovereignty framework that is not honest about its own limits is not a sovereignty framework. It is a new form of the problem it claims to solve.

---

## 7. A Call to Build

The Cortex Protocol is an open-source framework for human sovereignty in the age of AI. We invite developers, psychologists, neuroscientists, legal scholars, and people who simply believe their nervous system belongs to them.

The architecture of the security layer — how to protect the human, when to intervene, under what ethical constraints — is documented, versioned, and open.

What we need now are people who can close the gap between specification and evidence: the first clinical study, the first real sensor integration, the first Governance Node.

**Our minds are not products. Our sovereignty is non-negotiable. Our honesty about what we can and cannot guarantee is the condition that makes both of those things credible.**
